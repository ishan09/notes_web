First Principles Thinking: Reusable Prompts

Overview​

First principles thinking involves breaking down complex problems into fundamental truths and reasoning up from there. This document provides structured prompts for different scenarios.

1. General Problem-Solving Prompt​

Use this for any problem where you need to challenge assumptions and find innovative solutions.

PROBLEM: [Describe your problem]

STEP 1 - List Current Approach/Assumptions:
- What is the conventional way of solving this?
- What assumptions am I making?
- What constraints do I believe exist?

STEP 2 - Identify First Principles:
- What are the fundamental truths that cannot be disputed?
- What physical/logical laws govern this problem?
- What is objectively true vs. what is inherited wisdom?

STEP 3 - Question Each Assumption:
For each assumption above:
- Is this actually true, or just convention?
- Why do I believe this?
- What evidence supports/contradicts this?

STEP 4 - Rebuild From Fundamentals:
- Based on first principles alone, what solutions emerge?
- What would I do if no solution existed before?
- What new possibilities open up?

STEP 5 - Evaluate New Solutions:
- Does this solution violate any first principles?
- Is it practically feasible?
- What trade-offs exist?

2. Technical/Engineering Problem Prompt​

Use this for software architecture, system design, or technical decision-making.

TECHNICAL PROBLEM: [Describe the technical challenge]

STEP 1 - Current State Analysis:
- What is the standard industry approach?
- What technologies/patterns are typically used?
- What are the stated reasons for this approach?

STEP 2 - Fundamental Requirements:
- What problem are we ACTUALLY trying to solve?
- What are the core functional requirements?
- What are the true non-functional requirements (scale, performance, availability)?
- Remove all "nice-to-haves" - what is essential?

STEP 3 - Constraint Reality Check:
- Team size: [X developers]
- User scale: [Y users]
- Growth rate: [Z% per month/year]
- Budget: [$]
- Timeline: [timeframe]

STEP 4 - Physics/Logic of the Problem:
- What are the computational/network/storage fundamentals?
- What laws govern performance (CAP theorem, latency, bandwidth)?
- What is theoretically possible vs. practically feasible?

STEP 5 - Build From Zero:
- If I were solving this for the first time, what would I do?
- What is the simplest solution that addresses the fundamentals?
- Can I start simple and evolve?

STEP 6 - Validate:
- Does this solution meet the fundamental requirements?
- What assumptions am I still making?
- What could go wrong?

3. Learning/Understanding Prompt​

Use this when trying to deeply understand a concept, technology, or domain.

TOPIC TO UNDERSTAND: [Name the concept/technology]

STEP 1 - Strip Away Abstractions:
- What is this concept really about at its core?
- Remove all jargon - how would I explain it to a 10-year-old?
- What problem was this invented to solve?

STEP 2 - Historical Context:
- What existed before this?
- What problems did the old way have?
- What fundamental insight led to this solution?

STEP 3 - Core Principles:
- What are the 3-5 fundamental principles this is built on?
- What is essential vs. what is implementation detail?
- What physical/logical truths govern this?

STEP 4 - Build Mental Model:
- If I were to recreate this from scratch, what would I need?
- What are the irreducible components?
- How do these components interact at a fundamental level?

STEP 5 - Test Understanding:
- Can I explain this without using domain-specific jargon?
- Can I derive common patterns from the fundamentals?
- What would I do differently if starting fresh today?

4. Career/Business Decision Prompt​

Use this for strategic decisions about career moves, business strategy, or investments.

DECISION: [Describe the decision you're facing]

STEP 1 - Conventional Wisdom:
- What do most people do in this situation?
- What does industry/society say is the "right" path?
- What am I expected to do?

STEP 2 - Fundamental Goals:
- What am I actually trying to achieve? (Be specific)
- What does success look like in 5 years? 10 years?
- What are my core values and constraints?
- What resources do I have? (time, money, skills, network)

STEP 3 - Challenge Assumptions:
- "I should do X because everyone does" - Is this true?
- "I need Y to succeed" - Is this actually required?
- "Z is impossible" - Is it really, or just uncommon?

STEP 4 - First Principles Analysis:
- What are the fundamental economic/value principles at play?
- What creates actual value in this domain?
- What are the immutable constraints vs. perceived ones?

STEP 5 - Alternative Paths:
- Given my goals and first principles, what options exist?
- What unconventional paths might work better?
- What would I do if I had to create my own path?

STEP 6 - Risk Assessment:
- What is the worst-case scenario?
- Is it reversible?
- What is the opportunity cost of conventional vs. alternative paths?

5. Cost/Resource Optimization Prompt​

Use this when trying to reduce costs or optimize resource usage.

OPTIMIZATION GOAL: [What do you want to optimize?]

STEP 1 - Current Cost Breakdown:
- What am I currently spending? (money/time/resources)
- What is the standard/expected cost in this domain?
- Why is it expensive?

STEP 2 - Decompose to Components:
- What are the fundamental components/materials/inputs?
- What does each component actually cost at the base level?
- What is the markup at each layer?

STEP 3 - Value Chain Analysis:
- Where is value actually being added?
- Where is cost being added without value?
- What intermediaries exist? Are they necessary?

STEP 4 - Fundamental Requirements:
- What am I actually trying to achieve?
- What is the minimum viable solution?
- What features/components are truly essential?

STEP 5 - Alternative Approaches:
- Can I source/build components differently?
- Can I eliminate intermediaries?
- Can I change the process to be more efficient?
- What would this cost if I started from scratch?

STEP 6 - Calculate New Economics:
- What is the theoretical minimum cost?
- What is practically achievable?
- What trade-offs am I making?

6. Debugging/Root Cause Analysis Prompt​

Use this when troubleshooting problems or investigating failures.

PROBLEM/BUG: [Describe the issue]

STEP 1 - Observed Symptoms:
- What exactly is happening?
- When does it occur?
- What error messages/behaviors do I see?

STEP 2 - Strip Away Assumptions:
- What do I assume is causing this?
- What have others told me might be the cause?
- Set aside all theories - what do I actually KNOW?

STEP 3 - Fundamental Truths:
- What are the physical/logical laws governing this system?
- What MUST be true for the system to work?
- What is mathematically/logically impossible?

STEP 4 - Trace Back to Basics:
- What are the fundamental inputs to this system?
- How should they transform at each step?
- Where is the actual vs. expected diverging?

STEP 5 - Hypothesis from First Principles:
- Given the fundamentals, what could cause this?
- What is the simplest explanation?
- What can I test to prove/disprove this?

STEP 6 - Verify:
- Does my hypothesis align with all observed symptoms?
- What would definitively prove this?
- What am I still assuming?

7. Quick Daily Decision Prompt​

Use this for smaller daily decisions where you want to avoid cargo culting.

QUESTION: [Your question or decision]

1. What is the conventional/popular answer?

2. What am I actually trying to achieve?

3. What are 2-3 fundamental truths about this situation?

4. Does the conventional answer actually serve my goals based on these truths?

5. What would I do if no "standard way" existed?

Tips for Effective Use​

Do:​

  • Write down your answers - thinking on paper reveals assumptions
  • Be brutally honest about what you truly know vs. assume
  • Question everything, especially "best practices"
  • Start with "what is physically/logically possible?"
  • Consider extreme constraints (e.g., "what if I had 1/10th the budget?")

Don't:​

  • Skip steps - the process is important
  • Accept "because that's how it's done" as an answer
  • Ignore practical constraints entirely
  • Assume experts are always right
  • Stop at the first unconventional idea

Warning Signs You're Not Thinking from First Principles:​

  • "Industry standard is..."
  • "Everyone knows that..."
  • "We've always done it this way..."
  • "Best practice suggests..."
  • "No one does it differently..."

Examples of First Principles Questions​

Technology:​

  • Why do we need a database? (Could we use files? Memory? What's the fundamental need?)
  • Why microservices? (What problem are we solving? Is it real for us?)
  • Why this framework? (What does it actually provide that we can't do simpler?)

Business:​

  • Why do we need an office? (What's the fundamental purpose of co-location?)
  • Why 9-5 hours? (What actually needs to be synchronous?)
  • Why monthly releases? (What's the real constraint on deployment frequency?)

Learning:​

  • Why 4-year degree? (What skills do I actually need? How else can I get them?)
  • Why read the whole book? (What specific knowledge am I seeking?)
  • Why follow this curriculum? (What are my actual learning goals?)

Meta-Prompt: Creating Your Own Prompts​

When you need a prompt for a new domain:

1. What type of problem is this? (technical, strategic, analytical, creative)

2. What assumptions do people typically make in this domain?

3. What are the fundamental truths that govern this domain?

4. What questions would force me to confront those assumptions?

5. How can I structure the process: observe → question → decompose → rebuild?

Further Reading​

  • Aristotle's "Posterior Analytics" - Original source of first principles
  • Elon Musk interviews - Modern application in business/engineering
  • "Thinking in Bets" by Annie Duke - Decision-making from fundamentals
  • Feynman's approach to physics - Scientific first principles thinking

Remember: The goal isn't to reject all conventional wisdom, but to understand WHY things work the way they do, so you can determine if that wisdom applies to YOUR specific situation.
